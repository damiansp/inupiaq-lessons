Auto-Assimilator

In Iñupiaq, words are frequently constructed by adding multiple suffixes or postbases to root words. However, only certain combinations of sounds are allowed, so frequently when postbases are added, one or more letters in the new word changes in a process called asssimilation. Technically, assimilation is the process in which one sound becomes more like, (or assimilates to another sound.

There are a number of assimilation rules in Iñupiaq, and they can be challenging for the beginner to learn. But, with practice, the patterns will become more familiar. In the early stages though, you may check your assimilation rules here!

(Assimilation rules are not presented in this prototype, but would be introduced in a fuller curriculum.)

For example, the combination -ql- is not permitted in Iñupiaq. Instead, the q assimilates to the l by changing to a ġ. So, when the word "aġnaq" (woman), combines with the postbase "+lu" (and), the new word is "aġnaġlu" (and the woman).

A slightly more complicated assimilation occurs when adding "qimmɪq" (dog) and "+lu". Like the previous example, -ql- changes to -gl-, forming "qimmɪġlu", but we're not quite done. The "strong i" (represented here with the small capital "ɪ") causes the l to assimilate to ḷ, giving the final form "qimmiġḷu" (and the dog).

Try it out

Insert roots and postbases or endings in order, separated by commas, for example: "aġnaq, lu", "qimmɪq, lu", or "tikɪt, tuq", or simply combine them without assimilating like "aġnaqlu", "qimmɪqlu", or "tikɪttuq" then push the button to get the correct assimilated form. (Omit connectors like "+", "-", etc.)

The Present Indicative

In Iñupiaq, the present tense is expressed by adding different endings or conjugations to the verb root. In English present tense conjugations typically only have two form, one for he, she, or it, and one for all other subjects. For example, the verb "eat" adds an "-s" for the he/she/it form: "he eats, she eats, it eats", and nothing is added for all other subjects: "I eat, you eat, we eat, they eat, etc."

Iñupiaq verb endings distinguish between both "person" and "number". When talking about languages, "person" refers to who is speaking, being spoken to, or being spoken about. The first person refers to the speaker(s), the second person refers to the person/people being spoken to, and the third person refers to a person/people who are neither speaking nor being spoken to. "Number" refers to the number of people. Whereas English grammatical number only distinguishes between singular and plural, Iñupiaq makes a distinction between singular (1), dual (2), and plural (3 or more).

The subject of the verb is the person, animal or thing doing the action. Iñupiaq subjects can be any combination of person and number, making nine different categories all together. They are represented in the following table.

Subject Types in Iñupiaq

SingularDualPlural
1st PersonIWe (the two of us) We (the three or more of us)
2nd PersonYou (one person) You (two)You (all--three or more)
3rd PersonHe/She/It They (those two) They (those three or more)

As a shorthand, this site will use "s" for "singular", "d" for "dual", and "pl" for "plural" when needed.
